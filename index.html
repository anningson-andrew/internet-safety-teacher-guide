Internet Safety: Background

In our digital world, it is important that teachers talk with students about how to stay safe online. Just as we strive for schools to be safe and caring places, the internet also needs to feel safe for all students.

Canadian young people are among the most connected in the world. According to MediaSmarts' Young Canadians in a Wired World research, nearly all youth aged 9–17 are online daily, and a growing number report negative experiences including cyberbullying, unwanted contact, and exposure to harmful content.

At the same time, the internet is built on the same principles as our face-to-face communities — respect, responsibility, and the ability to navigate conflict. These skills are just as essential online as they are in the classroom or on the playground.

Source Criticism
Purpose
Increased awareness of source criticism and misinformation
Grade
Gr. 7Gr. 8Gr. 9
Outcome
Citizenship 9 — Unit 4ICT — Outcome 5
Source Criticism & Media Literacy
1

Watch Binogi's lesson film on Source Criticism, complete the accompanying quiz, and review key vocabulary as a class.

Let students discuss in pairs:

  • Have you ever shared something online that turned out to be false? What happened?
  • How do you decide whether a source is trustworthy?

Four-corners activity: Ask "Who is most responsible for stopping the spread of misinformation?" Students move to a corner: Individual users / Social media platforms / Government / Schools. Each group defends their choice, then the class discusses.

Explore MediaSmarts.ca for additional Canadian-context source-criticism resources and lesson extensions.

Greater understanding of bullying patterns and healthy relationships online
Grade
Gr. 7Gr. 8Gr. 9

Cyberbullying & Online Harassment
2

Watch Binogi's Bullying and Harassment film. Pause at each bullying pattern — verbal bullying, peer pressure, denial, indirect bullying — and discuss as a class or in small groups.

Bullying patterns to identify from the film:

  • Verbal bullying: Sarcasm used to belittle.
  • Peer pressure: Group laughter that isolates the target.
  • Denial: "We were just joking" used to minimize harm.
  • Indirect bullying: Witnessing and doing nothing.

Follow with Binogi's Cyberbullying film. Discuss: Why might someone behave more cruelly online than face-to-face? What would you do if a friend told you they were being cyberbullied?

Explore MediaSmarts' Let's Talk About… Online Harassment for Canadian case studies and extension activities.

Digital Sources
Purpose
Understand how digital footprints shape identity and opportunity
Grade
Gr. 7Gr. 8Gr. 9
Outcome
Citizenship 9 — Unit 4ICT — Outcome 2
Digital Footprint & Anonymity
3

Watch Binogi's Digital Sources film. Focus discussion on the concept of the digital footprint — what it includes, who can see it, and how long it lasts.

Discussion questions:

  • What would an employer, a university, or a stranger be able to find out about you from your online activity today?
  • Is anonymity online always a bad thing? Can you think of a situation where it protects someone?
  • If you could remove three things from your digital footprint right now, what would they be?

Connect directly to Citizenship 9, Unit 4: "Investigate how a digital footprint influences others' perceptions" and "Investigate the role of anonymity in both the production and consumption of media."

Consent
Purpose
Knowledge of consent and Canadian law around sharing images online
Grade
Gr. 8Gr. 9
Outcome
Citizenship 9 — Unit 4ICT — Outcome 2
Consent, Privacy & Images Online
4

Watch Binogi's Consent film. Review key vocabulary and complete the quiz.

Discussion questions:

  • How can you be sure someone has consented to having their photo shared, if they haven't said so in words?
  • In Canada, sharing intimate images without consent is a criminal offence (Criminal Code s. 162.1). Why do you think Parliament made this a law?
  • What would you do if a friend received an unwanted intimate image?

Use scenario cards in small groups to apply consent principles to realistic situations. Explore the Canadian Centre for Child Protection (cybertip.ca) for student-facing reporting resources.

Note for teachers: Before beginning this lesson, read the full guidance below and inform your school counsellor. Some students may have lived experience of image-based abuse. Follow NS Safe and Caring Schools protocols throughout.

Norms & Laws Conflict Management
Purpose
Understand who shapes norms and laws for online spaces
Grade
Gr. 7Gr. 8Gr. 9
Outcome
Citizenship 9 — Unit 4 & 5Social Studies 7–8
Norms, Laws & Responsibility Online
5

Watch Binogi's Norms and Laws film and the Conflict Management film. Complete the quizzes and review concepts.

Group discussion — who is most responsible for creating safe online spaces?

  • Lawmakers and government
  • Social media platforms and tech companies
  • Parents and teachers
  • Each individual user

Each group defends a position, then the class votes and discusses. Connect to Citizenship 9, Unit 5: "Evaluate ways to work within and outside of government structures to affect change."

Extension: Australia has introduced a social media ban for users under 16. Should Canada do the same? Students write a brief position statement and share with the class.
